01

Why Mathematics Is the Language of Machine Learning

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Most ML textbooks either skip the math or assume you already know it. This book fills that gap. The authors argue that without understanding the mathematical foundations — linear algebra, calculus, probability — you can use ML tools but cannot truly understand or improve them. Mathematics is not decoration; it is the engine inside every algorithm.
🧒 Plain explanation
🎵Imagine learning to play music. Some people just press buttons on a toy keyboard (they use ML tools). Others learn to read sheet music and understand chords — they can play any instrument and even write songs. This book teaches you the "sheet music" of machine learning.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
machine learning机器学习Teaching computers to learn from data without being explicitly programmed
algorithm算法A step-by-step procedure a computer follows to solve a problem
mathematical foundations数学基础The core math concepts everything else is built upon
practitioner从业者Someone who applies ML tools in real work
abstraction抽象化Hiding technical details to make something easier to use
✏️ Grammar note
"bring … to the fore" — This is a fixed English phrase meaning "to put something into a prominent or important position." Fore means "front" (as in "foreground"). The authors write: "This book brings the mathematical foundations… to the fore."
Pattern: bring [something] to the fore → make [something] visible and important
02

Linear Algebra — The Language of Data Shapes

📘 What the authors want to teach you
All data in ML — images, text embeddings, sensor readings — is stored as vectors and matrices. Linear algebra teaches us how to add, stretch, rotate, and transform these objects. The key ideas are: what a vector space is, how to solve systems of linear equations, and how linear transformations (matrices) reshape data.
🧒 Plain explanation
📦Think of a vector as a list of numbers (like coordinates on a map: "3 steps right, 5 steps up"). A matrix is like a grid of numbers that, when you multiply it by a vector, moves or stretches it in space. When ML models process an image, they treat it as a huge list of numbers and use matrix operations millions of times per second.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
vector向量An ordered list of numbers representing direction and magnitude
matrix矩阵A rectangular grid of numbers; performs transformations
scalar标量A single number (no direction)
vector space向量空间A collection of vectors where addition and scaling always stay inside the collection
linear independence线性无关No vector in a set can be built by combining the others
basis基底A minimal set of vectors from which all others in the space can be built
linear mapping / transformation线性映射A function between vector spaces that preserves addition and scaling
system of linear equations线性方程组A set of equations like 2x+y=5, x−y=1 to be solved simultaneously
rank秩The number of truly independent directions in a matrix
✏️ Grammar note
"such that" — In mathematics, "such that" introduces a condition or constraint. It means "with the property that" or "in a way that."
"Find a vector x such that Ax = b." → Find x which satisfies the condition Ax = b.
This is very different from everyday English where "such that" is rare. In math texts, you will see it constantly.
03

Analytic Geometry — Measuring Distance and Angle

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Vectors alone can't tell us how similar two things are. Chapter 3 adds geometry: inner products let us measure the length of a vector and the angle between two vectors. This is how ML measures similarity — for example, recommending movies by the "angle" between a user's preference vector and a film's feature vector. Projections let us find the closest point on a line (essential in regression).
🧒 Plain explanation
📐If two arrows point in almost the same direction, the angle between them is small — that means the two things are similar. If they point in completely different directions (90°), they are unrelated. The "dot product" is a number that captures this angle. Search engines and recommendation systems use exactly this idea to match your query to relevant results.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
inner product / dot product内积 / 点积Multiplies two vectors to give a single number capturing angle and length
norm (length)范数（长度）The "size" of a vector; computed from the inner product
orthogonal正交的Perpendicular; two vectors at exactly 90° — their dot product is 0
projection投影Finding the "shadow" of one vector onto another
rotation matrix旋转矩阵A matrix that rotates vectors without changing their length
cosine similarity余弦相似度Measuring similarity via the cosine of the angle between vectors
✏️ Grammar note
Passive voice in definitions — Math textbooks use passive voice heavily: "The norm is defined as…", "A basis is said to be orthonormal if…". This removes the subject (the mathematician) and makes the definition feel universal and objective.
Active: "We define the norm as ‖x‖." → Passive: "The norm is defined as ‖x‖."
When reading, translate passive voice back to "someone defined it this way."
04

Matrix Decomposition — Taking Matrices Apart to Understand Them

📘 What the authors want to teach you
A matrix can be broken into simpler pieces — like factoring the number 12 into 3×4. The most important decompositions are eigenvalue decomposition (finding the natural "stretching directions" of a transformation) and Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) (a universal tool that works on any matrix and underlies PCA, recommender systems, and data compression).
🧒 Plain explanation
🔬When you squash or stretch a rubber band, it moves in one direction more than others. Eigenvectors are those special "natural directions" of a matrix. SVD is like taking any shape and finding its skeleton — it reveals what a matrix really does. Netflix used SVD to discover hidden patterns in how millions of people rate movies.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
eigenvalue特征值How much a transformation stretches in a special direction
eigenvector特征向量The special direction that only gets stretched (not rotated) by a transformation
determinant行列式A single number describing how much a matrix scales volumes
SVD (Singular Value Decomposition)奇异值分解Breaks any matrix into three simple matrices; reveals structure
diagonalization对角化Rewriting a matrix in its own natural coordinate system
trace迹Sum of the diagonal entries of a matrix
symmetric matrix对称矩阵A matrix equal to its own transpose: A = Aᵀ
✏️ Grammar note
The prefix "eigen-" — This comes from German, meaning "own" or "characteristic." In English it is kept as a loanword because there's no clean translation. You'll see it only in linear algebra. Similarly, "Ansatz" (an educated guess/approach) and "Zerlegung" (decomposition) appear sometimes in advanced texts.
eigenvalue = the matrix's "own" value · eigenvector = the matrix's "own" vector
05

Vector Calculus — Finding Which Way Is "Uphill"

📘 What the authors want to teach you
To train a ML model, you need to minimize an error function (the "loss"). Calculus gives you the gradient — a vector that points in the direction of steepest increase of a function. By going in the opposite direction (gradient descent), you find the minimum. The chain rule allows you to differentiate complex nested functions — the backbone of neural network training (backpropagation).
🧒 Plain explanation
⛰️Imagine you're lost in the mountains in the fog. You want to reach the valley (lowest point = smallest error). You feel the ground under your feet — wherever it slopes downward most steeply, you walk that way. The gradient is like a compass that always points uphill; you do the opposite. That's gradient descent — the engine behind training every neural network.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
gradient梯度A vector of partial derivatives; points in direction of steepest increase
partial derivative偏导数How much a function changes when one variable changes, holding others fixed
chain rule链式法则Rule for differentiating a function inside another function: d/dx[f(g(x))]
Jacobian雅可比矩阵Matrix of all partial derivatives of a vector-valued function
loss function损失函数Measures how wrong a model's predictions are
gradient descent梯度下降法Repeatedly moving opposite to the gradient to minimize a function
backpropagation反向传播Applying chain rule backwards through a neural network to compute gradients
✏️ Grammar note
"with respect to" — This is one of the most common phrases in mathematics. It means "treating [X] as the variable." It signals which variable you are differentiating or measuring against.
"The derivative of L with respect to θ" = how L changes as θ changes (everything else is fixed).
You will see "w.r.t." as its abbreviation constantly in papers and textbooks.
06

Probability Theory — Quantifying Uncertainty

📘 What the authors want to teach you
The real world is noisy and uncertain. ML models must not only make predictions but also express how confident they are. Probability theory provides the framework: random variables, probability distributions (Gaussian, Bernoulli, etc.), and Bayes' theorem — which is how a model updates its beliefs when it sees new data. The Gaussian (normal) distribution is central because of its mathematical convenience.
🧒 Plain explanation
🎲Imagine a weather forecast: "70% chance of rain." It doesn't say "it WILL rain" — it gives a probability. ML models do the same for their predictions. Bayes' theorem is the rule for updating your belief: if you thought it was 60% likely to rain and then you see dark clouds, you update to 90%. This is how spam filters learn — starting with a guess, then updating with evidence.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
random variable随机变量A variable whose value is determined by a random process
probability distribution概率分布A function describing the likelihood of each possible value
Gaussian / normal distribution高斯分布 / 正态分布The famous bell curve; described by mean and variance
mean (expected value)均值（期望值）The average value of a random variable
variance方差How spread out the values are around the mean
Bayes' theorem贝叶斯定理Rule for updating probability given new evidence: P(A|B) = P(B|A)P(A)/P(B)
prior / posterior先验 / 后验Belief before / after seeing data
likelihood似然度How probable the observed data is under a given model
conditional probability条件概率Probability of A given that B has already occurred: P(A|B)
✏️ Grammar note
The vertical bar "|" — reading "given" — In probability, P(A | B) is read as "the probability of A given B." The word "given" here means "assuming B is already true / has already happened." This is not the same as "if" in ordinary English — it implies B is certain, not just possible.
P(rain | dark clouds) = probability of rain, given that we already see dark clouds.

Optimization — Finding the Best Answer

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Machine learning training is fundamentally an optimization problem: find the model parameters that minimize the loss function. Chapter 7 covers convex optimization (where you are guaranteed a global minimum), gradient descent variants, and constrained optimization (Lagrange multipliers) — used when parameters must satisfy certain conditions.
🧒 Plain explanation
🏆Imagine trying to find the deepest point of a hilly landscape blindfolded. If the landscape has only one valley (convex), you can always find the bottom by walking downhill. If it has many hills and valleys, you might get stuck in a small dip and miss the real deepest point. Optimization is the science of finding the true lowest point, reliably and efficiently.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
optimization优化Finding the input that makes a function as small (or large) as possible
convex function凸函数A "bowl-shaped" function with only one minimum
local minimum局部最小值The lowest point in a neighborhood, but not necessarily globally
global minimum全局最小值The absolute lowest point of the entire function
gradient descent梯度下降Step-by-step movement opposite to the gradient
learning rate学习率How large each step is during gradient descent
Lagrange multiplier拉格朗日乘子A technique for optimization with equality constraints
stochastic gradient descent (SGD)随机梯度下降Gradient descent using a random sample of data at each step
✏️ Grammar note
Infinitive vs. gerund after verbs — Notice: "we aim to minimize" vs "we avoid getting stuck." In mathematical English, aim/seek/want/need are followed by "to + verb." Verbs like avoid/consider/discuss take "verb+ing." This pattern is very consistent in academic writing.
✓ "We seek to find parameters that minimize the loss."
✓ "We avoid overfitting by regularizing."

The Three Pillars of ML — Data, Models, and Learning

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Every machine learning system consists of three things: (1) data — observations from the world; (2) a model — a mathematical structure that approximates the pattern in the data; and (3) a learning algorithm — the procedure that fits the model to the data by adjusting parameters to minimize prediction error on unseen data, not just training data.
🧒 Plain explanation
🍳Think of baking. The data is your ingredients. The model is the recipe (a specific structure). Training is adjusting the recipe — adding more sugar, less salt — until the cake tastes good. But the real test is whether someone who has never tried YOUR cake (unseen data) also loves it. That's called generalization.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
training data训练数据Examples the model learns from
test data测试数据Unseen examples used to evaluate how well the model generalizes
parameters参数The adjustable numbers inside a model (e.g., weights in a neural network)
overfitting过拟合Model memorizes training data but fails on new data
generalization泛化Performing well on unseen data — the true goal of ML
hyperparameter超参数Settings chosen before training (e.g., learning rate, number of layers)
cross-validation交叉验证A technique to estimate generalization by testing on multiple data splits
✏️ Grammar note
"guard against" — This phrasal verb means "to protect or defend against something bad." The authors write: "guidelines for building experimental set-ups that guard against overly optimistic evaluations."
"Guard against overfitting" = take steps to prevent overfitting from happening.
Other similar expressions: hedge against (protect financially), insure against (formal), safeguard against.

Linear Regression — Fitting a Line Through Data

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Linear regression is the simplest ML model: predict a number by fitting a straight line (or hyperplane) through data. The book teaches three levels: maximum likelihood estimation (find the line that makes the data most probable), MAP estimation (add prior knowledge to avoid extreme solutions), and Bayesian regression (maintain a full probability distribution over possible lines, not just one best guess).
🧒 Plain explanation
📈If you plot house prices against house size, you'll see a rough straight line trend. Linear regression finds the best line through those dots — the one that is, on average, closest to all the data points. The "Bayesian" version is more careful: instead of one line, it keeps track of ALL plausible lines and their probabilities — so you get not just a prediction but also an uncertainty range.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
linear regression线性回归Modeling the relationship between input and a continuous output with a line
maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)最大似然估计Choose parameters that make the observed data most probable
maximum a posteriori (MAP)最大后验估计MLE + a prior belief about what good parameters look like
Bayesian inference贝叶斯推断Maintaining a full probability distribution over parameters
least squares最小二乘法Minimizing the sum of squared differences between predictions and truth
regularization正则化Adding a penalty for large parameters to prevent overfitting
noise / residual噪声 / 残差The unexplained difference between the model's prediction and the true value
✏️ Grammar note
"integrate out" — In Bayesian statistics, "we integrate the parameters out" means mathematically removing them from the equation by summing over all their possible values. In everyday English, "integrate" means to bring things together — here it has a very specific calculus meaning.
"Instead of optimizing, we integrate the parameters out." = We don't pick one value for the parameters; we consider all values by integrating over them.

PCA — Compressing High-Dimensional Data

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Real-world data has thousands of dimensions (e.g., a 28×28 image = 784 numbers). Most of those dimensions carry redundant information. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) finds a small number of directions that capture most of the variation in the data. It uses eigenvectors to rotate the data into a new coordinate system where the most important dimensions come first.
🧒 Plain explanation
📸Imagine photographing a 3D object. A good photo taken from the right angle can tell you almost everything about the object's shape using just 2 dimensions. PCA finds the best "camera angle" for your data — the direction that shows the most variation, then the next best, and so on. You can then describe your data with far fewer numbers without losing much information.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
PCA (Principal Component Analysis)主成分分析Technique to reduce dimensions by finding directions of maximum variance
dimensionality reduction降维Representing high-dimensional data with fewer dimensions
principal component主成分A direction of maximum variance in the data; an eigenvector of the covariance matrix
covariance matrix协方差矩阵A matrix summarizing how each pair of dimensions varies together
variance方差How much data spreads in a particular direction
projection投影Mapping data onto a lower-dimensional subspace
reconstruction error重建误差How much information is lost when you compress and then decompress the data
✏️ Grammar note
Noun stacking — Technical English often stacks many nouns together without connectors: "high-dimensional data representation," "covariance matrix eigenvector decomposition." Read these from right to left: the last noun is the main thing; earlier nouns describe it.
"high-dimensional data representation" = a representation [of data] [that is high-dimensional]

Gaussian Mixture Models — Discovering Hidden Groups

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Sometimes data comes from several different subgroups (e.g., customer types, species of plants) but the labels are hidden. A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) assumes the data is a blend of several Gaussian distributions. The EM algorithm (Expectation-Maximization) iteratively guesses which group each point belongs to and then refines the group shapes — like sorting a mixed bag of coins without seeing their faces directly.
🧒 Plain explanation
🌌Imagine you're looking at stars through a telescope. Some stars form one cluster, others form another. You don't have labels — you just see a cloud of dots. GMM says: "I'll assume there are K groups. Let me figure out where the groups are and how big they are." It keeps refining its guess until the groups make sense. This is unsupervised learning — learning without labels.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
density estimation密度估计Learning the probability distribution that generated the data
Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)高斯混合模型A model that represents data as a mixture of several Gaussian distributions
EM algorithm期望最大化算法Iterative method: E-step (guess assignments), M-step (update parameters)
unsupervised learning无监督学习Learning patterns from data without labeled examples
cluster簇 / 类A group of data points that are similar to each other
latent variable潜变量A hidden variable that is not directly observed but influences the data
mixture weight混合权重How much each Gaussian component contributes to the overall distribution
✏️ Grammar note
"iterative scheme" — An iterative process is one that repeats a cycle of steps, improving with each repetition. A scheme in British academic English means a method or plan (not a devious plan as in American English). You'll see "iterative scheme," "iterative procedure," and "iterative algorithm" used interchangeably.
"We discuss an iterative scheme to find the parameters of this model." = We explain a method that repeatedly updates the parameters until they converge.

Support Vector Machines — Drawing the Best Boundary

📘 What the authors want to teach you
Classification assigns a label (e.g., spam / not spam, cat / dog) to each data point. Support Vector Machines (SVMs) find the hyperplane that separates two classes with the maximum margin — the widest possible gap between the boundary and the nearest points of each class. This makes the classifier robust. The kernel trick extends SVMs to non-linearly separable data.
🧒 Plain explanation
⚔️Imagine drawing a line on a map to separate two countries. Many lines could separate them, but the wisest boundary gives the most space (buffer zone) to both sides. That's exactly what SVMs do — they find the line (or plane) with the biggest buffer. Points right on the edge of the buffer are the "support vectors" — they're the hardest cases and they define the boundary.
🔑 Key terms
English中文Meaning
classification分类Assigning data points to discrete categories/labels
SVM (Support Vector Machine)支持向量机Classifier that finds the maximum-margin separating hyperplane
hyperplane超平面A flat boundary in high dimensions (a line in 2D, a plane in 3D)
margin间隔The gap between the decision boundary and the nearest data points
support vectors支持向量The data points closest to the decision boundary that define it
kernel trick核函数技巧Implicitly mapping data to a higher-dimensional space to make it linearly separable
supervised learning有监督学习Learning from labeled training examples
decision boundary决策边界The surface that separates different class regions
✏️ Grammar note
"require special care" — This is a polite academic way of saying "this is tricky and you must be careful." Authors use it to signal that the upcoming concept is subtle or that common intuition may mislead you. Equivalent phrases: "warrants attention," "deserves careful treatment," "must be handled with care."
"Unlike regression, where labels were real-valued, labels in classification are integers, which requires special care." = Watch out — integer labels behave differently and need different mathematical treatment.
